In basketball, we hear about \u201cthrees\u201d all of the time.\u00a0 A player lines up behind the arc, lets it go, and drains a three pointer.\u00a0 This past week, \u201cthrees\u201d took on a new meaning.\u00a0 When the big news was supposed to be about the NBA (finally) starting its season, the stories were much more grave.<\/p>\n

LaMarcus Aldridge of the Portland Trailblazers was diagnosed with Wolff-Parkinson-White Syndrome.\u00a0 He underwent a medical procedure.\u00a0 Jeff Green of the Boston Celtics discovered an aortic aneurysm and underwent surgery to correct the problem.\u00a0\u00a0 Chuck Hayes of the Sacramento Kings failed his physical due to a heart condition.\u00a0 In a league of 450 players, almost one percent of them discovered a potentially fatal heart condition in the last week.<\/em><\/p>\n

The NBA is no stranger to these heart conditions – Yinka Dare (died), Eddy Curry (surgery), Reggie Lewis (died), Ronny Turiaf (surgery), Kevin Duckworth (died), Cuttino Mobley (retired), Etan Thomas (surgery), Jason Collier (died), Robert Traylor (died), Zeijko Rebraca (retired), Fred Hoiberg (surgery) and Mikahil Torrance (retired).<\/p>\n

These players had access to the best medical care.\u00a0 Some of them were screened, some treated, and unfortunately, some died.\u00a0 Seven years ago, our son, Simon, died suddenly.\u00a0 He wasn\u2019t a basketball player; he wasn\u2019t screened; he was three months old.\u00a0 Through testing, we discovered that he, like many of these athletes, had a heart condition.\u00a0 His was Long QT Syndrome.<\/p>\n

Following his death, we started to pay attention to the stories about the thousands of other kids — infants, toddlers and teens — who drop dead every year from sudden cardiac arrest. \u00a0\u00a0The kids are sleeping in their cribs.\u00a0 They are swimming with friends.\u00a0 They are driving their car.\u00a0 They are playing sports.\u00a0 Their deaths are often times chalked up to SIDS, drowning, fatal auto accident and\/or dehydration.<\/p>\n

Sudden cardiac arrest is the indisputable leading cause of death of adults in the U.S.\u00a0 It is also kills the most student athletes every year.\u00a0 If our children are going to face these realities, why aren\u2019t we checking hearts and saving lives?<\/p>\n

In 2006, the NBA was the first league to institute a standard cardiac screening for all of its players.\u00a0 As a result, the stories are less tragic.\u00a0 Like the stories from last week, conditions are being detected and treated; fewer players are dropping dead.<\/p>\n

Back in the 1980\u2019s, the Italians started screening the hearts of all school-aged children.\u00a0 As a result, they reduced the incidents of sudden cardiac death in kids by 89%.\u00a0 Today, our organization, Simon\u2019s Fund, along with other organizations around the country, are providing heart screenings to tens of thousands of students every year.\u00a0 Collectively, we are finding that one out of every 100 students we screen has an undetected and potentially fatal heart condition.\u00a0 These are reasons enough to begin screening our children\u2019s hearts.<\/p>\n

We screen our kids at birth for dozens of rare genetic conditions.\u00a0 At school, all of our children get their eyes examined and hearing checked. \u00a0It\u2019s time for us to follow in the footsteps of the NBA and the Italians and start checking our kids\u2019 hearts too.<\/p>\n
