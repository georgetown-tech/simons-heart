Last Friday, I spent one day at two conferences about sudden cardiac arrest and death in the young.\u00a0 The SADS Foundation (www.sads.org<\/a>) hosted its Seventh Annual International Conference.\u00a0 SADS is dedicated to raising awareness about and researching arrhythmias.\u00a0 The Second Annual Connor Senn Symposium was a CME about causes and advances in the area of sudden death in students.\u00a0 I am proud to be associated with both organizations.<\/p>\n

Peter Mohler, MD, a researcher at Ohio State, and one of the organizers of the Connor Senn Symposium, concluded the day by making a simple observation.\u00a0 We continue to debate the value of using 1800 technology (EKG) to screen kids. \u00a0“Science is letting us down,” he said.<\/p>\n

It’s not just science, though. \u00a0It is the establishment too. \u00a0Every conference on SCA and the young that I’ve ever attended has staged this circus act. \u00a0The two conferences that I just attended did. \u00a0The American Heart Association<\/a> and American College of Cardiology<\/a> do it every year. \u00a0The American Academy of Pediatrics<\/a> just did it at its annual conference.\u00a0 It goes like this . . .<\/p>\n

In this corner, a well-published cardiologist using a few studies that compiled Google searches and insurance death records will pretend that these are really good sources, and prove beyond a reasonable doubt that EKG testing is expensive, inaccurate and unnecessary.<\/p>\n

In the other corner, another well-published cardiologist using the Italian study from 1982 that reduced sudden death in the young by 89% and some anecdotal data collected from community screenings in the United States will prove beyond a reasonable doubt that EKG screenings are affordable and save lives.<\/p>\n

After the circus is over, both sides go back to their practices and corners, and students continue to die from detectable and preventable conditions.<\/p>\n

You won’t be surprised to find out where Simon’s Fund stands in this debate. Based on the data we’ve gathered from our heart screenings, and the data shared by other organizations, cardiac screenings of our youth saves lives.\u00a0 They are accurate and affordable.<\/p>\n

You may be surprised, however, to hear us say, “don’t take our word for it.” We want science to settle this debate and we know that this requires data and proof.<\/p>\n

Here’s our proposal. We’ll raise the money. We’ll review all of the cardiology conference agendas from the last two years and invite the debaters to participate in a study. Yes, the biggest opponents and staunchest proponents side by side. Maybe we’ll make it a reality TV show too.<\/p>\n

They will collectively decide on how many students are needed for the sample, and they’ll screen kids, together. \u00a0That’s right, Dr. No Screening will sit right beside Dr. Mandatory Screening and review the results.<\/p>\n

There will be no retrospective analyses, Google searches or foreign studies. \u00a0We’re going to put electrodes on students in this country and see how this plays out. \u00a0We\u2019ll do complete physicals, medical histories and EKG exams on all of the students.\u00a0 We\u2019ll do echos on some.<\/p>\n

In the end, one hypothesis will prevail, but we can settle the debate using science and patient research.<\/p>\n

Are you in? \u00a0It may force you to get down from your soapbox and\/or cut down on your presentations. \u00a0It may force conference planners to conjure up other controversial topics. \u00a0It may even save some lives.<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
