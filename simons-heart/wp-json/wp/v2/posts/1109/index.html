Health Screening Report<\/p><\/div>\n

It’s all good.\u00a0 The Pennsylvania School Health Code required a health screening for our daughter, Sally.\u00a0 During the school day, she participated in a battery of tests.\u00a0 Her vision is good.\u00a0 Her hearing is good.\u00a0 Her height, weight and body mass index are all good.\u00a0 She can see and hear, but could she die?<\/p>\n

The CDC estimates that 1 in every 1000 children suffer from hearing loss<\/a>.\u00a0\u00a0It also\u00a0suggests that vision loss <\/a>is not very common in kids – more common in adults.\u00a0 So, if the incidents of hearing and vision loss (or impairment) are so low in children, why does the State mandate testing in these areas?<\/p>\n

There are probably a few reasons, but I imagine the most obvious is that\u00a0kids with\u00a0unknown hearing and\/or vision problems have\u00a0trouble learning.\u00a0 Therefore, early detection is important.<\/p>\n

At Simon’s Fund, we believe that 1 out of every 100 children have a potentially fatal heart condition.\u00a0 This is validated by studies<\/a> that have been conducted in Europe, and from the data gathered at our own screenings (check out our counter on the home page).<\/p>\n

So, why doesn’t the State include an ECG exam in the health screening?\u00a0 It is not expensive and it is no more invasive than a hearing or eye exam.\u00a0 Here’s how it differs though . . . this test can save lives.<\/p>\n

As parents, we want to know that our children won’t have any obstacles to learning.\u00a0 However, we also want to\u00a0know that our children will be alive to learn.\u00a0 Every year, thousands of kids drop dead from sudden cardiac arrest.\u00a0 The heart is a pretty important organ.\u00a0 Let’s stop taking it for granted.<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
