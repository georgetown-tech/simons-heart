I grew up in Ohio. I graduated from Ohio State. I met my wife there. \u00a0I love the Buckeyes.\u00a0 However, today, as they hold the funeral service for Ryan Fischer<\/a>, my heart is in Michigan.\u00a0 Ryan was Grandville\u2019s hockey team captain and he died from an enlarged heart on the eve of the state championship.<\/p>\n

Michigan is no stranger to losing students to heart conditions.\u00a0 In 2013, it was track runner, Mario Campbell<\/a>.\u00a0 In 2012, it was a kindergartner<\/a> at Field Day.\u00a0 In 2011, it was high school basketball standout, Wes Leonard<\/a>, who collapsed and died after making the winning shot.<\/p>\n

Leonard\u2019s death received a lot of media attention and Fischer\u2019s is too.\u00a0 That\u2019s what happens when our most gifted athletes, our children, collapse and die for no apparent reason, from conditions that are detectable and treatable.<\/p>\n

Following Wes\u2019 death, this headline read, \u201cStudy finds student athlete sudden cardiac deaths like Fennville star Wes Leonard are as rare a deaths from lightening.\u201d<\/a>\u00a0 The headline was based on a paper appearing in Circulation<\/i>, an American Heart Association publication, and was titled Sudden Death in Young Competitive Athletes \u2013 Analysis of 1866 deaths in the United States, 1980 \u2013 2006<\/i><\/a>.<\/i><\/p>\n

According to a cursory Google search, at least four Michigan students have died from sudden cardiac arrest in the last two years.\u00a0 It is probably more accurate to say that four deaths have been picked up by the media. \u00a0So, what is the real number?\u00a0 How many children die from sudden cardiac arrest?<\/p>\n

This lightning analogy appeared in Circulation, <\/i>a publication of the American Heart Association in 2009.\u00a0 The death count was rooted in the US National Registry on Sudden Death in Athletes, a creation of the Minneapolis Heart Institute Foundation.\u00a0 This \u201cregistry\u201d is based on LexisNexis, Burrelle\u2019s Information Services, Internet searches, reports from various governmental agencies and independent organizations.<\/p>\n

I am shocked (no pun intended) that this resource can be identified and accepted as a registry in the medical world.\u00a0 The term registry connotes official, legitimate, scientific and comprehensive. \u00a0At best, these sources offer an unscientific and incomplete glimpse of the picture. \u00a0They are better suited for students completing research papers.\u00a0 To use this \u201cregistry\u201d as the basis to influence healthcare policy and debate a standard of care is misleading and reckless.<\/p>\n

Case in point.\u00a0 Nicholas Bennett, a student athlete from Ionia Middle School (also Michigan), died September 25, 2013.\u00a0 At the time, his obituary<\/a> was the only record of his passing, and it did not reference sudden cardiac arrest.\u00a0 It wasn\u2019t until the passing of Ryan Fischer, six months later, that we learned more about Nicholas from this article<\/a>.\u00a0 How many more Nicholases are not being counted?<\/p>\n

This \u201cregistry\u201d suggests that approximately 72 student athletes die nationwide every year.\u00a0 So, how many student athletes are dying from lightning strikes?<\/p>\n

According to the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, seven<\/a> people died from lightning in the State of Michigan between 2003 – 2012.\u00a0 To put another way, 0.7 people died each year from lightning.\u00a0 By the way, all seven were adults.<\/p>\n

This debate isn\u2019t about Michigan though.\u00a0 It is about the United States and this number doesn\u2019t take into account states like Florida and Texas that have many more lightning deaths.<\/p>\n

The American Academy of Pediatrics estimates that 2,000 children<\/a> die in the U.S. every year from sudden cardiac arrest.\u00a0 When extrapolated over a fifty-three year period, this number is 106,000.<\/p>\n

During this same period (1959 \u2013 2012), the NOAA recorded 4,002 deaths<\/a> from lightning strikes, and the overwhelming majority of the deaths were adults.<\/p>\n

The conditions that cause our children to drop dead are detectable and treatable.\u00a0 We have a cure . . . it\u2019s called prevention.\u00a0 While it is tragic to read about the deaths of Wes, Nicholas, Ryan and Mario, the real tragedy lies within a very well respected segment of the medical community that confuses and misguides the public about the incidents of sudden cardiac arrest and death in children.\u00a0 If we can\u2019t rely on them to help us define the scope of the problem, we\u2019ll never solve the problem, and we\u2019ll continue to bury children like Ryan.<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
