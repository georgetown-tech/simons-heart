Published by the Huffington Post<\/em><\/p>\n

I love this time of year. My colleagues strut around the office pretending to know who is going to win the NCAA Tournament. They spend countless hours on the web studying bracketolgy, only to lose to someone who knows nothing about basketball, but likes the names of some schools more than others. You guys know who you are. Yes, we are in the midst of March Madness.<\/p>\n

Despite what the nickname suggests, it isn’t all madness. Some things have become very predictable. You must be at least an\u00a0eight seed<\/a>\u00a0to win. A twelve seed\u00a0always upsets<\/a>\u00a0a five seed. No team has ever\u00a0gone undefeated<\/a>\u00a0and won the tournament. Basketball players are at the\u00a0greatest risk<\/a>\u00a0of sudden cardiac arrest.<\/p>\n

It’s true. Heart conditions and sudden cardiac arrest have become just as much a part of the NCAA tournament as Cinderella stories and Big Dances. This year, at least five teams faced this start reality.<\/p>\n

Jay Simpson\u00a0<\/a>(Purdue) was forced to retire earlier this season due to hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM).\u00a0\u00a0Justin Moss<\/a>\u00a0(Buffalo) was diagnosed with hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, but was able to continue playing because he received an implantable cardioverter defibrillator (ICD).\u00a0\u00a0DJ Bowles<\/a>\u00a0(Wichita State) collapsed on the basketball court during his freshman year, two years ago, and was forced to retire. DJ actually played along side\u00a0Carl Hall<\/a>, another Shocker, diagnosed with a heart condition.\u00a0Dwayne Polee<\/a>\u00a0(San Diego State) was diagnosed with a cardiac arrhythmia earlier this year, but was cleared to return.\u00a0\u00a0Tyler Adams<\/a>\u00a0of Georgetown was forced to retire three years ago after discovering a heart condition. He graduates this season.<\/p>\n

During this mad month, we are inundated with statistics. However, no one is talking about this one – eight percent of the teams in this year’s tournament could have seen their teammate collapse and die on the court. So why aren’t we hearing more about this threat?<\/p>\n

Actually, we finally are. Earlier this month, Dr. Brian Hainline, the first Chief Medical Officer of the NCAA\u00a0told the world<\/a>\u00a0that basketball players, and other high risk athletes, should be required to undergo an EKG exam prior to participating in sports. This is a tremendous development in amateur sport.<\/p>\n

For years,\u00a0professional sports<\/a>\u00a0leagues have been checking the hearts of their athletes. Last year, we were reminded about the benefit of this policy.\u00a0Isaiah Austin<\/a>\u00a0(Baylor University) was disqualified from the NBA draft after being diagnosed with Marfan Syndrome. In addition to stories, there is research to show that an EKG, coupled with a physical exam and medical history, is the\u00a0most effective way<\/a>\u00a0to detect heart conditions that can lead to sudden cardiac arrest. They’ve been doing it right for professionally athletes. We are now one step closer to taking care of our student athletes too.<\/p>\n

For years, my nonprofit organization,\u00a0Simon’s Fund<\/a>, which was founded in memory of my three-month old son, Simon, has provided free heart screenings for students. We find that approximately one out of every 100 students has an undetected heart condition. We are one of many organizations doing this kind of work. Check out\u00a0Screen Across America<\/a>\u00a0to find the others.<\/p>\n

In 2013, while Louisville, Michigan, Syracuse and Wichita State were facing off at the Final Four, Simon’s Fund was up the road in Gwinnett County conducting a heart screening with the\u00a0CardioVascular Group<\/a>. Earlier that year, two student athletes from Buford, both basketball players, died from sudden cardiac arrest —\u00a0Jeremy Nelson<\/a>and\u00a0Adam Smith<\/a>. They will never travel down the road to the Final Four. That’s when we realized the importance of connecting the Final Four to the cardiac safety of our student athletes.<\/p>\n

This year, Simon’s Fund will be in Indianapolis with\u00a0Giving Hearts a Hand<\/a>, the\u00a0John Stewart Foundation<\/a>,\u00a0Play for Jake Foundation<\/a>\u00a0and\u00a0Riley Hospital for Children at IU Health<\/a>\u00a0to provide free heart screenings for high school students in Marion County. Every student who attends the screening at Crispus Attucks Medical Magnet High School will receive a physical exam, EKG and echocardiogram.<\/p>\n

If Kentucky goes on to win this year, we will all witness history. It will be the first time that a national champion has\u00a0gone undefeated<\/a>\u00a0in the era of the sixty-four team tournament. However, the one shining moment of this March is clearly Dr. Hanline’s commitment to checking the hearts of our student athletes. Kentucky’s accomplishment will give us much to talk about in the decades to come. Dr. Hainline’s effort will save lives.<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
