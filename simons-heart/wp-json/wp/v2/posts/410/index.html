When I was a little boy, I remember\u00a0watching the show, That’s Incredible<\/a>.\u00a0\u00a0It featured\u00a0all kinds of unbelieveable or freakish things.\u00a0 I has very low expectations about the show’s credibility.\u00a0\u00a0I just wanted to be entertained.<\/p>\n

Today, I am attending the\u00a0Parent Heart Watch<\/a> conference.\u00a0 I sitting with about 30 other families that have lost children to sudden cardiac arrest.\u00a0\u00a0We are listening to doctors talk about the incidence of sudden cardiac arrest and death in children.\u00a0 Unlike the content of That’s Incredible, I expect that the facts and findings\u00a0from the medical community will be credible and based on scientific research.<\/p>\n

There is a pretty vigorous debate about the need for\u00a0ECG exams on our young athletes.\u00a0 There is the issue of cost, liability, false positives, and quite frankly, the number of children affected.\u00a0 This is a healthy debate, and as a parent who has lost a child, I recognize the validity\u00a0of the economic argument – “there just aren’t enough kids dying to spend the money.”\u00a0 However, this argument, when offered by a world renowned\u00a0medical expert, must be based on\u00a0scientific data that is credible and reliable.<\/p>\n

Barry Maron, M.D., Director of the Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy Center at the Minneapolis Heart Institute Foundation, issued a recent report <\/a>that found\u00a0that the incidence of sudden cardiac arrest in young athletes\u00a0is “relatively low.”\u00a0 His report\u00a0determined\u00a0that only\u00a01,866 young athletes\u00a0died from 1980 – 2006.\u00a0 His findings are based on\u00a0data found in a national registry.\u00a0 That means that about 66 student athletes die every year from sudden cardiac arrest.\u00a0 Hmmm . . . world renowned expert . . . national registry . . . findings, data,\u00a0studies, reports . . . case closed, right?\u00a0 Unless, you stop to ask yourself, “what\u00a0is this finding based on?”<\/p>\n

There is no national registry.\u00a0 The registry is based on data that was gleaned from newspaper articles and internet searches.\u00a0 If a newspaper didn’t carry the story, or the story did not show up in the search engines, the death was not reported and it did not end up in the “national registry.”\u00a0 Furthermore, the athletes did not include children engaged in recreational sports, like\u00a0Little League\u00a0or intramurals.<\/p>\n

This is the kind of research\u00a0that I\u00a0expect from\u00a010th graders in my U.S. History class.\u00a0\u00a0I couldn’t get them to stop using Google or Wikipedia.\u00a0\u00a0I have different much higher expectations of\u00a0medical experts that\u00a0influence public policy.\u00a0\u00a0Dr. Maron’s\u00a0finding should have been the basis of a hypothesis that “incidence of sudden cardiac arrest in the youth is relatively low.”\u00a0\u00a0He should have used that hypothesis to conduct a scienfic study\u00a0to support his hypothesis.\u00a0\u00a0Instead, he took\u00a0advantage of his clout and reputation, and offered the world\u00a0something much less, and now the truth will be that much harder to find.<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
